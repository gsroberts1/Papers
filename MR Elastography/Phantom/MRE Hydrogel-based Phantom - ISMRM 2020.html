Magnetic Resonance Elastography Hydrogel-based Phantom Fabrication
David R Rutkowski1,2, Grant S Roberts3, Charles C Hower4, Corinne R Henak2,4, Oliver Wieben1,3,4, and Alejandro Roldán-Alzate1,2,4
1Radiology, University of Wisconsin, Madison, WI, United States, 2Mechanical Engineering, University of Wisconsin, Madison, WI, United States, 3Medical Physics, University of Wisconsin, Madison, WI, United States, 4Biomedical Engineering, University of Wisconsin, Madison, WI, United States

Synopsis

Magnetic resonance elastography (MRE) allows for in-vivo tissue stiffness mapping. However, the multiple types of MRE methodologies and sequences, and a variety of MRE applications, warrant validation with representative phantoms. This preliminary study was aimed at developing an easy-to-manufacture, hydrogel-based phantom containing materials of varying stiffness (ranging from 2.5 kPa to 20 kPa), and validating the material through direct mechanical testing. MRE-obtained stiffness correlated well with preliminary data obtained through mechanical tests. Initial results show promise for highly-adaptable hydrogel phantoms. Further validation, including longitudinal testing and test-retest reliability, is needed to assess robustness.

INTRODUCTION

Magnetic resonance elastography (MRE) is increasingly used for non-invasive assessment of liver stiffness, and has applications in many other body regions, including kidney, prostate, breast, and brain tissue(1-4). Phantom studies are an essential component of validating MRE methodology developments such as accelerated acquisitions, inversion algorithms for the generation of stiffness maps, sensitivity to size of regions with varied stiffness, etc. A wide variety of phantoms have been proposed for MRE experimentation using materials such as silicone, polyvinyl alcohol, gelatin, agar, plastisol, and polyvinyl chloride, to mimic mechanical stiffness properties of human tissue(5-10). This project aims to fabricate a low cost and improved MRE phantom with segments of varied stiffness using adaptable hydrogels, so that a wide range of MRE protocols could be tested and validated.

METHODS

Phantom: An MRE phantom with 5 compartments of varied mechanical stiffness was fabricated with polyacrylamide hydrogel (Figure 1). The hydrogel material base-solution was created by dissolving two polymer components -acrylamide and bis-acrylamide(Fisher Scientific, Hampton, NH, USA)- into de-ionized water. The stiffness of the hydrogel samples was varied by changing the percentage of polymer components dissolved in the water base. Gel percentages of each sample included in the phantom were 4%, 6%, 8%, and 10%. Cross-linking of the gel was initiated by the addition of Tetramethylethylenediamine (TEMED) and ammonium persulfate (Fisher Scientific, Hampton, NH, USA).
Four hydrogel compartments were created by filling cylindrical molds with hydrogel of different stiffness properties (Figure 1A) and adding food coloring for easier idenification. Portions of each sample material, along with additional samples of 12% and 16% gel, were poured into a separate container for later mechanical testing. The four cylinders were placed in a large acrylic housing (8”x6”) and surrounded by a fifth hydrogel of 6% gel composition (Figure 1B). A cylindrical void of water was also included in the phantom. One week after initial scanning, the hydrogel rods and surrounding material were removed from the large acrylic housing and inserted into a smaller housing (Figure 1C) to examine the effects of housing size and thickness on MRE excitation and measurement. Remaining material gaps were filled with a second background hydrogel formulation of 6% gel.
MRI: MRE stiffness images were obtained using a single-shot spin echo EPI pulse sequence on a 3T imaging system (SIGNA Premier, GE Healthcare, Waukesha, WI) using a 30 channel AIR™ coil and 60-channel posterior coil: scan time=5min; TR=3000ms; TE=71ms; phase offsets=8; vibration frequency=60Hz; vibration amplitude=50% max; slice thickness = 3mm; matrix=80x80. Mechanical vibrations were introduced using a soft, pillow-like driver through a pneumatic actuator (Resoundant, Rochester, MN). The phantom was placed on the pillow driver such that the cylinders were running vertically. MRE stiffness maps were obtained using a 3D direct inversion reconstruction (4,11). Regions of interest (ROI) measurements were manually drawn over all cylinders (including background material) in Matlab (Mathworks, Natick, MA).
Mechanical Testing: The hydrogel samples that were set aside during phantom fabrication were mechanically tested in unconfined compression (12). Cylindrical samples were made using biopsy punches. Sample thickness was measured using calipers and samples were placed between two glass plates on a tabletop test machine equipped with a 1 kg load cell. A small tare load was applied, followed by a ramp to 10% strain at 0.10 s-1. Following testing, engineering strain and first Piola-Kirchhoff stress were calculated. A linear modulus was fit to the stress-strain data using custom MatLab code.

RESULTS

Two functional phantoms of different size and housing construction were successfully created and withstood mechanical MRE vibration in the full range of MRE excitations. The mean and standard deviation of measurements obtained from MRE at 2 time points and mechanical analysis measurements are listed in Table 1. Initial MRE scanning on the first phantom iteration produced material modulus results comparable to results produce by mechanical analysis (Figure 2). However, repeat MRE testing of the hydrogel one week after fabrication revealed changes in the phantom properties.

DISCUSSION

This preliminary study demonstrated the potential for constructing a hydrogel-based MRE phantom with targeted material properties at reasonable cost and manufacture time. The polyacrylamide gels used were highly adaptable, covering a wide tissue stiffness range. Furthermore, the high water content of these gels proved to be beneficial for general image quality with high signal. The acrylic phantom shell was also found to be beneficial, as it acted as a good medium to transfer excitation waves from the MRE driver to the internal hydrogel. One complication of this study was the change of phantom properties over the span of a week. We believe that this change was due to the manner of hydrogel storage and transfer of gels between housings, and can therefore be avoided through proper adaptations. Future work will include testing of alternative hydrogel formulations (including UV cured gels), investigation into the effects of phantom morphology on MRE performance, and enhancement of longitudinal robustness.

CONCLUSION

Phantoms provide reference standards for sequence development, study validation, and machine calibration. The ability to easily and inexpensively produce a phantom with objects of heterogeneous stiffness, such as the phantoms proposed in this preliminary study, may be of great value to the MRE community.
